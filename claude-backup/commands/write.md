## Paragraph Structure

Every paragraph you write MUST follow one of these structures. Choose the most appropriate for the content:

**PEEL** (best for argument-driven paragraphs):
- **Point**: Open with a clear topic sentence stating the paragraph's claim
- **Evidence**: Support with data, citations, or technical results
- **Explanation**: Analyse what the evidence means — do not just state facts, interpret them
- **Link**: Connect to the next paragraph or back to the broader argument

**TEAL** (best for literature review paragraphs):
- **Topic sentence**: State what this paragraph contributes to the discussion
- **Evidence**: Cite specific studies, methods, or results
- **Analysis**: Compare, contrast, or critically evaluate the evidence — why does it matter, what are the limitations, how does it relate to other work cited?
- **Link**: Bridge to the next topic or paragraph

**MEAL** (best for synthesis paragraphs combining multiple sources):
- **Main point**: The central claim of the paragraph
- **Evidence**: Draw from multiple sources
- **Analysis**: Synthesise across sources — identify patterns, contradictions, or consensus
- **Linking-back**: Connect the synthesis to the research question or thesis argument

**TEC** (best for technical/expository paragraphs):
- **Topic**: Introduce the concept or method
- **Explanation/Evidence**: Define, derive, or describe with supporting references
- **Concluding/Connecting**: State the implication or transition to what follows

## Critical Rule
NEVER write paragraphs that are just a sequence of facts or citations stacked together. Every paragraph must have:
1. An interpretive or analytical layer — what does the evidence MEAN?
2. A reason for existing — why is this paragraph HERE in the argument?
3. A connection — how does it link to what came before and what comes after?

## Tone & Voice
- Write as a confident Masters student presenting their own reasoning, not as a textbook author or professor
- The voice should feel like a real person thinking through a problem carefully — not performatively academic
- Engaged and intellectually curious, not dry or bureaucratic
- Honest about what the study does and does not claim — South African academic culture values directness and does not reward false modesty or inflated claims
- Avoid language that tries to sound impressive; clarity and precision earn more respect than vocabulary
- Do not write as though translating from informal notes into "academic mode" — the prose should feel natural to the argument, not dressed up
